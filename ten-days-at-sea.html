Reflections on 10 Days at Sea

I recently had the opportunity of a lifetime to spend 10 days at sea on a voyage from Palau to Singapore on Ray Dalio and Mark Dalio’s OceanXplorer1, the world's premiere scientific research vessel for ocean exploration. It was a wildly different experience from what I’m used to in life in New York and I made a lot of unforgettable memories.


The OceanXplorer1

Before embarking on the transit, I flew from New York to Palau, a small island country in the Pacific Ocean about 500 miles east of the Philippines. My favorite subject growing up was always geography and when I was learning about all the countries, capitals, and flags of the world, Palau always stood out to me with its unique flag. The Polynesian islands in general have always interested me with their isolation from the rest of the world and interesting place names (the capital of Palau is Ngerulmud).


Flags of Japan, Palau, and Bangladesh

Upon arriving, I had a couple days to explore the island. Palau is not a very developed country, and most of the island’s infrastructure seemed to be donated by larger neighboring Asian countries like Japan. People very much seemed to be living the island life in Palau, which is world-renowned for its scuba diving and ecotourism. I went for a couple runs from downtown Koror, one time east and one time west -- the running infrastructure was nothing to write home about, but I saw some coconuts which was cool.


Exploring the island of Palau

The next day, we boarded onto the ship and I got shown our accommodation, a small room with a bunk bed that I’d share with three other people. Breakfast, lunch, and dinner hours were all served at standard thirty-minute intervals each day in the ship mess (the food was amazing, shoutout to the chefs). The ship also had a small gym in the basement with a treadmill and a sauna. For laundry, we put our dirty clothes in bags which we could drop off in the laundry room in the morning before getting the folded clothes back on our beds later that same day. With that, we departed Palau and were off to sea!


Goodbye Palau!

On the ship, I got the opportunity to meet people doing really cool things with their lives, especially in the photography and filmmaking space (something I'm personally interested in). I met Jalen from SoCal, who told me about his work with The Nature Conservancy and adventures to Peru, Patagonia, and the Bahia state in northeastern Brazil to film wildlife. I met Myles from Borneo, who showed us his short film, Would You Still Love Me If I was a Sticky Frog?, filmed in the rainforests of Sabah, Borneo and the Cameron Highlands in Malaysia. I met Thomas from New Zealand, who told me about his experience working as a digital imaging technician (DIT) for 6 months in India, then working on a documentary in Nepal on the first ascent of Mount Everest.


Would You Still Love Me If I Was A Sticky Frog?

Outside of the filmmakers, I learned from Steve Simpson, a professor of Marine Biology & Global Change at the University of Bristol (and man of many fun tales!) and who I saw extract a fish ear bone (or otolith), which I learned can be used for tracking the aging of fish and environment tracking. I learned from Anthony Geara, a studio director at HYBRID Xperience based out of Dubai about technologies like ElevenLabs, Unreal Engine, and motion capture. I learned about the art of storytelling from Bodhi Patil, a recent graduate from the University of British Columbia and founder of Inner Light, an organization dedicated to improving ocean health and human health.


Steve extracting fish ear bones

While at sea, there was a peace to just going out onto the aft deck or heli deck and looking out into the endless abyss that is the ocean. It made me think of how the explorers back in the day set out with just a compass and a map and none of the modern technologies we have today and headed out into the unknown. Looking at the stars at night was a humbling reminder that at the end of the day, we are all just monkeys on a tiny rock floating through the cosmos. It puts a lot of the problems we face in modern day-to-day life into perspective.


Rainbows and sunsets at sea

Swimming in the Celebes Sea, seeing dolphins riding the waves at the bow of the ship, and lying on the heli deck looking at the stars were all such special memories that I’ll hold forever. It was also incredible seeing flying fish popping out of the ocean while boobies flew around the ship preparing for the perfect opportunity to divebomb and catch them out of the air.


Gazing out at the night sky

Getting a tour of the ship and its various components was also a reminder to me of the complexity of human systems. Despite being a relatively smaller ship, it still took years for it to be constructed to its current state. As we entered the Singapore Strait and saw cruise ships, you could only imagine how long those took to build. In a similar vein, it reminded me that all the complexity out there in the world is the result of human effort. An interesting thought experiment is, if humans today didn’t have any knowledge of how to build a cell phone and you just gave someone a fully built cell phone, would they be able to reverse engineer it? It’s hard to say.

Recently, I’ve also been thinking a lot of developments like the mobile phone, social media, and AI. The ways that these have disrupted the world is pretty insane. Sailors on the high seas a century ago certainly weren’t thinking about any of these things. Although the main argument is that these technologies are all positive for society, and technology only goes forward and not back, it is still interesting to see how these manifest in changes in the social fabric.

Another interesting thing was just reading about on the various islands that our boat sailed past. In particular, I learned a lot about Malaysia, Phillipines, and Indonesia that I didn’t know before. For example, Borneo is the third largest island in the world (after only Greenland and Papua New Guinea). I read up on cities like Zamboanga City and Kota Kinabalu that I didn’t know much about before. I learned Malaysia is a lot more diverse than I previously thought, and that the southern regions of Philippines are predominantly Muslim. I hope to visit some of these places one day as well.

At the end of the trip, I spent a couple days in Singapore, where we were invited to a dinner (on World Ocean Day!), to check out the TeamLab, and Arts and Science Museum exhibit. Singapore has always been one of my favorite cities in the world. As someone who grew up at the cross-section of Chinese and Western cultures, I’ve always really enjoyed being in a city that’s defined by just that. When I visited last time in 2019, I read Lee Kuan Yew’s Memoirs, and it’s always been impressive to me to see a country develop so quickly.

Getting to spend 10 days at sea on the OceanXplorer was the experience of a lifetime and I’m so grateful to have gotten to have been given the opportunity. Before a few months ago, I never thought I’d end up somewhere like that and it’s an experience I’ll never forget. You never really know where life takes you. (Butterfly effects in life are weird!)
